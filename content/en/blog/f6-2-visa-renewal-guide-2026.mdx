## What is the F-6-2 Visa?

The F-6-2 visa is a stay qualification established under Appendix 1, Item 28-2 of the Enforcement Decree of the Immigration Control Act. It is granted to individuals whose marriage to a Korean national has ended but who cannot maintain a normal marital relationship due to the fault of the Korean spouse, or who are raising a child born during the marriage. This is a humanitarian status that allows continued residence in Korea after divorce or the death of a Korean spouse.

## Who Qualifies for an F-6-2 Visa?

You may apply for F-6-2 if you fall under one of the following categories.

- **At-fault divorce**: Divorced due to the fault of the Korean spouse (domestic violence, abandonment, infidelity, etc.)
- **Child rearing**: Currently raising a child born from the marriage to a Korean national
- **Spouse's death**: Raising a minor child after the death of the Korean spouse

## When to Apply for Renewal

F-6-2 visa renewal (extension of stay period) applications may be submitted up to **4 months before** the expiration date.

- **Recommended timing**: 2 months before expiration
- **Minimum timing**: 1 month before expiration
- **Important**: Remaining in Korea after expiration is illegal overstay

## Required Documents

### Common Documents

1. **Stay period extension permit application form**
2. **Passport** (original and copy)
3. **Alien Registration Card**
4. **Application fee**: KRW 60,000
5. **One photo** (3.5cm × 4.5cm)
6. **Proof of residence**: Lease agreement, building owner confirmation, etc.

### Child Rearing Documents

- **Family relationship certificate** (detailed, including children)
- **Child's birth certificate** or birth registration
- **Child enrollment certificate** (school, kindergarten, daycare)
- **Child's health insurance card** or dependent confirmation
- **Child support payment documentation** (if applicable)

### At-Fault Divorce Documents

- **Court ruling on divorce** (including the section identifying fault)
- **Domestic violence damage confirmation** (if applicable): Police damage report, medical records
- **Written explanation or petition** explaining the grounds for fault

### Spouse's Death Documents

- **Death certificate** or family register deletion record
- **Child rearing documents** (same as above)

## Screening Criteria

Immigration offices evaluate the following comprehensively.

- Substantiality of child rearing (actual cohabitation with the child)
- Stability of life in Korea (income, stable residence)
- Explanation of grounds for fault divorce
- Criminal history and legal compliance
- Child's welfare and best interests

## Common Rejection Reasons

- Insufficient proof of actual child rearing
- Living separately from the child
- Loss of parental rights or custody
- Serious criminal record
- Illegal employment or other legal violations
- Child has reached adulthood (rearing relationship ended)

## Change of Status After Child Reaches Adulthood

When the child being raised reaches adulthood (age 19), the basis for maintaining F-6-2 status may cease. In this case, consider transitioning to the following statuses.

- **F-2 (Residence)**: Transition to residence visa if certain conditions are met
- **F-5 (Permanent Residence)**: If requirements for permanent residence are satisfied
- **Employment visa (E-series)**: If engaged in employment

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q. Can I get an F-6-2 visa without children after divorce?**
A. Maintaining F-6-2 based solely on at-fault divorce without children is difficult. At-fault divorce alone may qualify for F-6-2 application, but the screening criteria are strict — professional consultation is strongly recommended.

**Q. Can I work with F-6-2 status?**
A. Holders of F-6-2 status may work without a separate employment permit.

**Q. Can I transition from F-6-2 to permanent residence (F-5)?**
A. F-6-2 holders who meet a minimum residence period (typically 5 years) and other requirements may apply for F-5 permanent residence.

## Why income matters

The F-6 marriage visa is granted on the basis of marriage to a Korean citizen, but the Korean spouse must also demonstrate the **financial capacity** to support a family after the foreign spouse arrives. Each year the Immigration Office publishes a minimum threshold, and the applicant must prove that household income for the previous 12 months meets it.

## Income that counts

In principle, only **income reported to the National Tax Service** is recognized.

- Employment income (withholding tax statement, payroll records)
- Business income (comprehensive tax return, certificate of income amount)
- Real-estate rental income
- Interest and dividend income (within certain limits)
- Pension income

## Income that does not count

The following items are excluded or only partially recognized:

- Unreported cash income
- One-off proceeds from selling assets
- Occasional remittances from relatives
- Cryptocurrency trading profits (some immigration offices currently do not accept these)

<Callout tone="info" title="Household combination">
The income of parents, siblings, or children is not added by default, but in some cases co-sponsorship can be used. The eligibility and limits depend on the specific case and require prior review.
</Callout>

## Standard documents

Income proof typically includes the following set:

1. **Certificate of income amount** (issued via Hometax, covering one year)
2. **Comprehensive income-tax return** or **withholding-tax statement**
3. **Employment certificate or business registration certificate** (current activity proof)
4. **Health-insurance enrolment certificate** and **payment record**
5. **Bank statements** (3–6 months, showing income inflows)

## Case-specific reinforcement

- **Self-employed**: VAT base certificate, business expense and rental flow records
- **Freelancers**: Client contracts, tax invoices, and recurring deposit records
- **Pension recipients**: Pension statement and continuity proof

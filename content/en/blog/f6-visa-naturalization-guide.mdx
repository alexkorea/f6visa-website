# How to Apply for Korean Naturalization on an F-6 Visa — Complete Guide

If you hold an F-6 (marriage immigration) visa and have decided to build your life permanently in Korea, **Korean naturalization** is the most comprehensive path available to you. It grants full citizenship — including voting rights, unrestricted residency, and the ability to pass Korean nationality to your children.

This guide covers everything F-6 holders need to know: eligibility requirements, the step-by-step application process, dual citizenship rules, and a detailed comparison with F-5 permanent residency.

---

## 1. Can F-6 Visa Holders Apply for Naturalization?

Yes. Korea's Nationality Act provides a **simplified naturalization (간이귀화)** pathway specifically for foreigners married to Korean citizens. Compared to general naturalization (which requires 5+ years of continuous residence), the simplified route is significantly more accessible for marriage immigrants.

The two core prerequisites are:

- **A valid marriage**: You must be legally married to a Korean citizen, or — if the marriage has ended — be raising a minor child of the Korean spouse in Korea.
- **Residence requirement**: You must have resided in Korea for at least 1 year after marriage (conditions vary by type — see below).

---

## 2. Simplified Naturalization Requirements

Under Article 6 of the Nationality Act, simplified naturalization requirements are as follows.

### 2-1. Marriage and Residence Requirements

| Type | Marriage Duration | Domestic Residence |
|---|---|---|
| **Type A** | 2+ years since marriage | 1+ year continuous residence in Korea after marriage |
| **Type B** | 3+ years since marriage | 1+ year residence in Korea after marriage (overseas periods may be counted cumulatively) |

> **Example**: Married in January 2023 → Moved to Korea in January 2024 → Eligible to apply from January 2025 (Type A)

### 2-2. Legal Capacity and Good Conduct

- Must be 19 years of age or older (adult)
- No criminal record (checked both domestically and internationally)
- No unpaid taxes or financial penalties

### 2-3. Financial Self-Sufficiency

- Must demonstrate ability to support yourself through personal or spousal income
- Receipt of government welfare is not an automatic disqualifier, but ability to sustain independent living must be shown

### 2-4. Korean Language Ability

- Completion of **Korea Immigration and Integration Program (KIIP) Level 4 or higher**, or
- **TOPIK (Test of Proficiency in Korean) Level 1 or higher** (or equivalent)
- A basic conversational Korean ability is also assessed during the naturalization interview

### 2-5. Understanding of Korean Society and Culture

- Must pass the **KIIP comprehensive evaluation (귀화시험)** or qualify for an exemption
- Exemptions apply to: those who have completed KIIP Level 5, certain international students, and other designated categories

## 3. Naturalization Application Process

Applications are submitted through your **local immigration office (출입국·외국인청)** and processed by the **Ministry of Justice (법무부)**. The total processing time is approximately **1 to 2 years**.

### Step-by-Step Overview

```
Step 1: Complete KIIP (Korea Immigration and Integration Program)
     ↓
Step 2: Submit naturalization application at local immigration office
     ↓
Step 3: Document review + background check by Ministry of Justice
     ↓
Step 4: Naturalization interview
     ↓
Step 5: Naturalization approval notification
     ↓
Step 6: Register acquisition of nationality + Renounce foreign nationality
```

### Required Documents

- Naturalization application form (available at immigration office)
- Passport copy + Alien Registration Card copy
- Marriage certificate (including Korean spouse's family relations certificate)
- Criminal background check from home country (with Apostille or consular authentication)
- Entry/exit record certificate (출입국사실증명서)
- Proof of residence (lease agreement, resident registration, etc.)
- Proof of financial sufficiency (income tax certificate, business income records, etc.)
- Korean language proficiency proof (KIIP completion certificate or TOPIK score report)
- 2 passport-sized photos

> Document requirements may change. Always confirm the current checklist with your local immigration office before applying.

## 4. Dual Citizenship — What You Need to Know

Korea generally **does not allow dual citizenship**. After naturalization is approved, you must **renounce your original nationality within 1 year**. Failure to do so will result in cancellation of the naturalization approval.

### Exceptions to the Dual Citizenship Rule

Under Article 10, Paragraph 2 of the Nationality Act, dual citizenship (multiple nationality) may be retained in certain cases that can apply to F-6 holders:

| Exception | Description |
|---|---|
| Naturalization at age 65 or older | Exempted from nationality renunciation due to age |
| Raising a minor child born to a Korean citizen | Exception may be granted in the interest of the child's welfare |
| Legal/practical impossibility of renouncing foreign nationality | E.g., where the home country's law automatically strips citizenship upon naturalization |

> **Important**: Dual citizenship exceptions are assessed strictly on a case-by-case basis. Confirm your eligibility in advance with a certified immigration specialist or directly with the Ministry of Justice.

---

## 5. Naturalization vs. F-5 Permanent Residency — Which Is Right for You?

Naturalization is not the only long-term path. **F-5 permanent residency** is a strong alternative that lets you retain your original nationality. Compare the two options below.

| Factor | Naturalization (Korean Citizenship) | F-5 Permanent Residency |
|---|---|---|
| Nationality change | Acquire Korean nationality, renounce original | Retain foreign nationality |
| Length of stay | Unlimited (as a citizen) | Unlimited (periodic renewal required) |
| Voting rights | Yes | No |
| National health insurance | Equivalent access | Equivalent access |
| Children's nationality | Korean nationality can be conferred | Separate procedure required |
| Extended overseas stay | No restriction | PR may lapse after 2+ years abroad |
| Processing time | Approx. 1–2 years | Approx. 6 months–1 year |
| Nationality renunciation | Required (with limited exceptions) | Not required |

**Summary**: If you plan to fully settle in Korea for life, naturalization provides the most comprehensive status. If you want to maintain ties to your home country and keep your original passport, F-5 may be the better fit.

## 6. FAQ

**Q1. Can I apply for naturalization without an F-6 visa?**
A. You must hold a valid residency status when applying. F-6 is the most common route, but other visa types may also qualify if you meet all the requirements.

**Q2. What happens if my visa expires while my naturalization application is pending?**
A. Applicants whose naturalization is under review can apply for a stay extension specifically for naturalization applicants. Contact your local immigration office for details.

**Q3. Can I still apply after divorce?**
A. Yes. Under Article 6, Paragraph 2 of the Nationality Act, if you are raising a minor child born to your Korean ex-spouse, you may still apply for simplified naturalization after the marriage ends.

**Q4. What does the naturalization interview involve?**
A. It is a one-on-one interview with a Ministry of Justice examiner, typically under 30 minutes. Topics include basic Korean conversation, your life in Korea, and your reasons for seeking naturalization.

**Q5. Do I have to surrender my foreign passport immediately after naturalization?**
A. No. You have up to 1 year after naturalization approval to complete the renunciation of your foreign nationality and report it to the Ministry of Justice. Contact your home country's embassy in Korea for the renunciation procedure.

**Q6. My Korean is not very strong. Is naturalization still possible?**
A. Yes. The KIIP program is designed to support marriage immigrants from beginner level. Completing the program satisfies the language requirement, so with consistent effort over 1–2 years of study, naturalization is fully achievable.

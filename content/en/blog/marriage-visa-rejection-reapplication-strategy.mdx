## What Does an F-6 Marriage Visa Denial Mean? — Legal Framework and Key Concepts

### Legal Basis for F-6 Visa Review

The F-6 marriage immigration visa is a residency status issued under Article 10 of the Immigration Act and Annex 1 of its Enforcement Decree.
It is granted to foreign nationals who are in a legally recognized marriage with a Korean citizen, and the review process is overseen by the [Korea Immigration Service, Ministry of Justice](https://www.immigration.go.kr).
The review is far more than a document check — it involves a comprehensive assessment of **the authenticity of the marriage**, **the sponsor's financial capacity**, and **the suitability of both parties**.

### Options After a Denial Notice

Even after receiving a denial, you can pursue a reapplication or file an objection.
However, reapplying with the same documents as before is very likely to result in another denial.
**You must analyze the reason for denial, address the deficiencies, and only then proceed with reapplication.**

> **Important:** The timing and any restrictions on reapplication after a denial vary depending on the reason and the specifics of your case.
> Always consult a professional to determine the right timing for reapplication in your situation.

## The #1 Reason for Denial — Insufficient Proof of a Genuine Marriage

### What Is Authenticity Review?

Immigration authorities assess not just whether a marriage exists on paper, but **whether the couple is genuinely living as a married couple**.
This is known as the marriage authenticity review, and it is applied with particular rigor to international marriages to guard against sham marriages.
Even if your paperwork appears formally complete, a finding of insufficient authenticity can result in denial.

### Common Cases Where Authenticity Is Called Into Question

The following situations may raise concerns about the genuineness of a marriage:

- The couple met very recently or has little documented history of contact
- The two parties share no common language for communication
- There is little to no messaging history via KakaoTalk, social media, or other platforms
- Family members are unaware of the spouse's existence
- A prior marriage exists but is not adequately explained

> **Practical tip:** In similar cases, applicants have successfully reapplied by submitting additional evidence such as communication records, photos, and records of mutual visits over the course of the relationship.
> Reach out for a consultation to learn exactly what materials to gather and how to present them most effectively.

## Missing or Incorrectly Formatted Documents — The Hidden Trap of Technical Denials

### Common Types of Document Errors

Document-related denials often have nothing to do with the substance of your case — they stem from **formatting errors, expired documents, or translation issues**.
Typical examples include missing apostilles, absent notarizations, uncertified translations, and documents submitted past their validity period.
A single missing or defective document can derail an entire application, so it's essential to understand the requirements for each item precisely.

| Document Type | Common Error | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Marriage certificate | Issued more than 3 months prior | Must obtain a freshly issued copy |
| Foreign government documents | Missing apostille or notarization | Requirements vary by country |
| Translated documents | Submitted without certified translation | Must be verified by a certified translator |
| Medical examination certificate | Issued by a non-designated facility | Must use an immigration-approved clinic |
| Income/employment verification | Missing official stamp | Must be reissued or supplemented |

### Country-Specific Document Requirements

Document requirements differ depending on the applicant's nationality.
Countries such as Vietnam, Thailand, China, and Russia each have distinct requirements for which documents to submit and how they must be notarized.
The [HiKorea portal](https://www.hikorea.go.kr) provides a general overview of country-specific requirements, but **the information may not reflect recent regulatory changes, so always verify directly with the relevant authority**.

## Failing to Meet Income Requirements — Financial Eligibility Standards

### Legal Basis for the Income Requirement

The Korean spouse (sponsor) must meet a minimum income threshold as part of the F-6 visa review.
This threshold is subject to **annual revision** under the relevant provisions of the Enforcement Rules of the Immigration Act, and the current standard can be confirmed through the [Korea Immigration Service, Ministry of Justice](https://www.immigration.go.kr).
Since the exact income requirement changes from year to year, please consult a professional to confirm the figure that applies to your case.

### Alternatives When the Income Requirement Is Not Met

Even if the sponsor's income falls short of the threshold, there may be ways to address this:

- Exploring whether real estate or financial assets can be submitted as supplementary evidence
- Considering whether income or assets from other family members can be taken into account
- Strategically timing the reapplication after income levels improve

> **Important:** The methods and scope of acceptable income supplements vary from case to case.
> Submitting incorrect or unsuitable documents can actually work against you, so always have a professional review your approach before proceeding.

## Criminal Background Checks — A Lesser-Known Reason for Denial

### Standards for Background Eligibility

The visa review process includes a **background check** on both the sponsor and the applicant.
Under Article 11 of the Immigration Act, certain criminal records may constitute grounds for entry prohibition or visa denial.
Convictions for offenses such as assault, drug-related crimes, fraud, or sexual offenses may have a negative impact on the review outcome.

### How to Handle a Criminal Record

Having a criminal record does not automatically mean your visa will be denied.
Reviewers take a holistic approach, considering the nature of the offense, the sentence imposed, whether it was carried out, and whether any probationary periods have elapsed.
Because outcomes vary greatly depending on the specifics, **a professional pre-assessment based on your particular situation is essential**.

## FAQ — Common Questions About Reapplying After a Marriage Visa Denial

**Q1. How long do I have to wait before reapplying after a marriage visa denial?**

The waiting period before reapplication depends on the reason for denial and the specifics of your case.
In some situations, you can reapply right away — but doing so without addressing the underlying issue greatly increases the likelihood of another denial.
A professional review of your denial notice is the best way to determine exactly when you are eligible to reapply.

**Q2. I wasn't given a written reason for my denial. What should I do?**

The way denial information is communicated varies depending on where you applied — whether through an embassy, an immigration office, or another channel.
Even when no reason is explicitly stated, there are administrative avenues — such as requesting disclosure of government information — that may allow you to find out more.
A professional can walk you through the specific steps available in your situation.

**Q3. Is it possible to get a marriage visa even if my income is below the required level?**

Falling short of the income threshold does not necessarily mean an automatic denial.
There may be ways to supplement the requirement using assets or income from family members, though this depends on the individual case.
A professional consultation is the best starting point for exploring your options based on your current financial situation.

**Q4. My application was denied in Korea — can I reapply through an overseas embassy instead?**

Which route is available to you depends on your current residency status.
The review standards and required documents can differ between the two pathways, so it's worth analyzing which one gives you a better chance.

# What Happens to Your F-6 Visa After Divorce in Korea

The F-6 marriage immigration visa is granted on the basis of a valid marriage to a Korean national. When a divorce is finalized, the legal foundation of the F-6 visa disappears — but **divorce does not mean you are immediately required to leave Korea.** Korea's Immigration Act provides three distinct pathways that allow F-6 holders to remain in the country after a divorce.

---

## 1. What Happens to Your F-6 Visa After Divorce?

From the moment a divorce becomes final — whether by mutual agreement (협의이혼) or by court judgment — your F-6 status loses its underlying legal basis. However, the Immigration Act and related regulations allow you to apply for a status change under the following circumstances:

| Situation | Condition | New Status |
|---|---|---|
| F-6-2 | You have custody/parental rights over a Korean-national child | F-6-2 (Child-rearing) |
| F-6-3 | The Korean spouse was at fault for the breakdown of the marriage | F-6-3 (Spouse's fault) |
| Other | You independently meet requirements for another visa category | E, D, F series, etc. |

If none of these conditions apply, you will generally need to **depart Korea** or convert to a short-term status to settle your affairs before leaving.

---

## 2. F-6-2 — For Those Raising Korean-National Children

### Eligibility
- You have been designated as the **custodial parent** (친권자 / 양육자) of a **Korean-national child** following the divorce.
- The child must currently reside and be raised in Korea.

### Application Process
1. Secure the divorce judgment or mutual-divorce confirmation that clearly specifies custody arrangements.
2. Visit your local **Immigration Office (출입국·외국인청)** and file a **visa status change application** to F-6-2.
3. Await the review decision (typically 1–2 months).

### Key Documents Required
- Visa status change application form
- Divorce judgment or mutual-divorce confirmation (court-issued)
- Custody/parental-rights documentation (stated in the judgment or a separate family court order)
- Child's Basic Certificate (기본증명서) and Family Relations Certificate (가족관계증명서)
- Alien Registration Card and passport
- Proof of residence (e.g., lease agreement)

> **Note:** F-6-2 status can be renewed as long as you continue to raise the child in Korea. When the child reaches adulthood or custody circumstances change, different rules may apply.

---

## 3. F-6-3 — When the Korean Spouse Was at Fault

### What Counts as "Fault"?
Under Enforcement Decree of the Immigration Act and Ministry of Justice guidelines, the following situations qualify:

- **Domestic violence** (physical, psychological, or financial abuse)
- **Desertion or abandonment** by the Korean spouse (disappearance, running away without notice)
- **Adultery or infidelity** by the Korean spouse leading to the breakdown of the marriage
- **Unilateral refusal to divorce** or **marriage entered into through deception** by the Korean spouse

> Evidence is everything. Gather police report records, medical certificates, text/KakaoTalk messages, and witness statements. The more documentation you have, the stronger your case.

### Application Process
1. Collect all available evidence of the Korean spouse's fault.
2. You can apply for F-6-3 **even while divorce proceedings are still ongoing** — it is recommended to simultaneously request a temporary stay extension.
3. Submit the application at your local Immigration Office.

### Key Documents Required
- Divorce judgment citing the Korean spouse's fault — or proof that proceedings are ongoing
- Domestic violence: 112 incident report, medical diagnosis certificate, restraining order, etc.
- Desertion: police report confirmation
- Other supporting evidence: message logs, witness affidavits
- Alien Registration Card, passport, and application form

## 4. Changing to a Different Visa Category

Even if you do not qualify for F-6-2 or F-6-3, you may be able to change to another status if you independently meet the requirements.

| Status | Condition | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **E series** (E-1 to E-7, etc.) | Employment requirements met (job contract, qualifications) | Employer sponsorship required |
| **D-2** (Student) | Admission to a Korean university or graduate school | Enrollment proof required |
| **D-10** (Job-Seeking) | Bachelor's degree or higher, or certain income history | Max 6 months; limited extension |
| **F-2** (Residence) | Long-term stay history, social integration program completion, etc. | Separate review process |
| **G-1** (Miscellaneous) | Humanitarian grounds, pending litigation, etc. | Short-term / temporary in nature |

If your divorce proceedings are still in progress, applying for **G-1 (Miscellaneous) status** as a temporary measure while you finalize your situation can be a practical option.

---

## 5. The 14-Day Reporting Obligation

Under Article 35 of the Immigration Act, foreign nationals must report any change in status that affects their visa **within 14 days** to the relevant immigration authority. Divorce qualifies as such a change.

- **Deadline:** Within 14 days of the divorce becoming final
- **How to report:** Visit your local Immigration Office in person, or use the HiKorea (하이코리아) online portal
- **Consequences of non-reporting:** Administrative fines and potential negative impact on future visa applications and renewals

> It is strongly recommended to file your status-change application **at the same time** as, or immediately after, submitting the divorce notification.

## 6. FAQ

**Q. My divorce isn't finalized yet. Do I need to leave Korea now?**
A. No. As long as your F-6 visa remains valid, you may continue to stay legally in Korea. After the divorce is finalized, report it within 14 days and begin the status-change process.

**Q. What if I couldn't apply for a status change before my F-6 expired?**
A. The Ministry of Justice may exercise discretion and allow a brief grace period. However, do not wait until you become an overstayer — contact your Immigration Office immediately and request guidance on available remedies.

**Q. My child is not a Korean national. Can I still apply for F-6-2?**
A. No. F-6-2 applies only to parents raising **Korean-national children**. If your child is not a Korean national, explore the F-6-3 or alternative status-change routes.

**Q. I am a domestic violence victim but I have very little evidence. What should I do?**
A. Call the Korea Women's Hotline (**1366**, available 24/7) or go to the nearest police station and file a report immediately. The police report record itself is important evidence. Then consult a licensed immigration scrivener (행정사) or attorney.

**Q. After getting F-6-3, can I eventually get permanent residency?**
A. F-6-3 holders can renew their status while their circumstances remain valid. Over time, depending on your overall stay period and other qualifications, you may be able to transition to F-2 (Residence) or F-5 (Permanent Residence). A case-by-case evaluation is needed.

Divorce from a Korean spouse does not automatically require you to leave Korea. If you are raising your Korean national child, the F-6-3 status allows you to stay and continue your parental role.

## What is F-6-3?

Under Korea's Immigration Control Act, F-6-3 is granted to **foreign parents raising a minor child who holds Korean nationality**. It applies even when the marriage has ended due to divorce, death of a spouse, or other circumstances.

## Eligibility Requirements

1. You are actually raising a Korean-national minor child
2. You hold custody or parental rights over the child
3. The child resides in Korea

If the other parent has sole custody or the child lives abroad, F-6-3 eligibility becomes significantly harder to establish.

## Required Documents

- Status change application form
- Passport and alien registration card
- Divorce judgment or agreed-divorce certificate
- Child's family relations certificate
- Custody or parental rights documentation
- Proof of actual cohabitation (resident registration, lease agreement)
- Income verification (to demonstrate ability to support the child)

## Application Process

```
Step 1: Complete divorce proceedings
         ↓
Step 2: Prepare custody documentation
         ↓
Step 3: File F-6-3 status change at Immigration Office
         ↓
Step 4: Review period (approximately 1–3 months)
         ↓
Step 5: F-6-3 status granted (renewed annually)
```

## Important Notes

Immigration officers focus on **actual custody** — not just paperwork. School enrollment records, medical records, and residence verification are all examined. F-6-3 must be renewed each year and expires when the child turns 19.

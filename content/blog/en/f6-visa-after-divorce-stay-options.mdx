# F-6 Visa After Divorce in Korea: Your Complete Guide

One of the most common questions from F-6 visa holders facing divorce is: "Will my visa be cancelled immediately?" The short answer is no — but you must act quickly and strategically. This guide explains every option available to you after divorce and what steps to take right away.

## 1. What Happens to Your F-6 Visa After Divorce?

The F-6 marriage visa is granted on the basis of an active marital relationship with a Korean citizen. Once divorce is legally finalized, the foundational reason for your F-6 status ceases to exist. However, this does not mean you are immediately deported.

Under Korean immigration law, you must take action — such as applying for a status change — within **14 days of your divorce being registered**. Failing to do so within this period may result in illegal overstay status, so prompt action is critical.

## 2. Staying for Child Custody (F-6-2)

If you have custody of a Korean child following divorce, you may be eligible to remain in Korea under the **F-6-2 status**, which applies to foreign parents raising the minor children of Korean nationals.

### Requirements for F-6-2
- Proof of finalized divorce (divorce confirmation, family relationship certificate)
- Evidence of child custody and actual caregiving (custody agreement, proof of cohabitation with child)
- Proof of financial ability to support the child (income or asset documentation)

### Important Notes
F-6-2 status is valid as long as you are actively raising the child. Once the child reaches adulthood, this basis for staying expires. Plan ahead by exploring long-term options such as F-5 permanent residency.

## 3. No-Fault Divorce Stay (F-6-3)

If the divorce was **caused by your Korean spouse's misconduct** — such as domestic violence, infidelity, or abandonment — you may qualify for the F-6-3 status. This provision specifically protects foreign spouses who are victims of their Korean partner's wrongdoing.

### Requirements for F-6-3
- Evidence that the Korean spouse was at fault (police reports, medical records, court rulings, domestic violence documentation)
- Documents showing you bear no fault for the divorce
- Proof of finalized divorce

### Duration
F-6-3 is typically renewed annually. It is a transitional status — for long-term residency, you will need to move to another visa category eventually.

## 4. Humanitarian Stay Permits

Even if you do not qualify for F-6-2 or F-6-3, the Minister of Justice has discretion to grant a **humanitarian stay permit** in certain situations, including:

- Victims of domestic violence
- Severe illness making immediate departure medically impossible
- Need to remain for ongoing legal proceedings in Korea

Humanitarian stays are granted at the government's discretion and are not guaranteed. Professional assistance significantly improves your chances.

## 5. Alternative Visa Options

If you plan to remain in Korea long-term after divorce, consider transitioning to one of these visa categories:

### E-7 Specific Activities Visa
If you qualify for a specialized profession recognized under Korean immigration law, the E-7 visa allows employment in that field. You will need a job offer and must meet the profession-specific requirements.

### D-10 Job-Seeker Visa
The D-10 visa allows you to stay temporarily while searching for employment. It has time limits, so you must convert to a work visa quickly once you secure a job.

### F-2 Residency Visa
If you have a significant history of lawful stay in Korea, have completed the Social Integration Program, or meet other qualifying criteria, the F-2 residency visa may be an option. F-2 holders have broad employment rights and greater flexibility.

### F-5 Permanent Residency
For those who have been in Korea for many years and meet the points-based or special criteria, F-5 permanent residency eliminates ongoing visa renewal concerns entirely.

## 6. What to Do Immediately After Divorce

Follow these steps as soon as your divorce is finalized:

1. **Schedule an immigration appointment**: Book within 14 days of divorce registration.
2. **Assess your situation**: Do you have child custody? Was the divorce your spouse's fault? Do you have employment options?
3. **Gather documents**: Divorce certificate, child-related paperwork, financial records, and any evidence of spousal misconduct.
4. **Consult a professional**: An immigration administrative agent can identify the best path for your specific circumstances.
5. **Apply for status change**: File for the appropriate visa category without delay.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q1. Is my status immediately illegal the day the divorce is finalized?**
A. No. You have a grace period, but you must take action within 14 days of the divorce being registered. Notify the immigration office and apply for a status change.

**Q2. My spouse was abusive but I have limited documentation. What can I do?**
A. Various forms of indirect evidence are accepted: police reports, hospital records, witness statements, and text messages. Contact the Danuri Helpline (1577-1366) for legal support for domestic violence victims.

**Q3. I have visitation rights but not custody. Can I apply for F-6-2?**
A. F-6-2 requires actual caregiving and cohabitation with the child. Visitation rights alone generally do not qualify. You will likely need to explore other visa options.

**Q4. What happens to my visa status while divorce proceedings are ongoing?**
A. Your F-6 status is not immediately cancelled during divorce proceedings. Keep the immigration office informed and apply for an extension of stay if needed while the case is in progress.

**Q5. Can I work while on F-6-3 status?**
A. F-6-3 may have employment restrictions. If you need to work, you may need to obtain separate authorization or transition to a work-eligible visa such as E-7.
